   - 毕业论文以及摘要：
    Tendon-Driven Mechanisms for Adaptive Robotic Grasping
    and Handheld Surgical Instruments
    Submitted by
    Yu WANG
    for the degree of Master of Philosophy
    at The University of Hong Kong
    in May, 2026
    This thesis studies tendon-driven mechanisms for adaptive robotic manipulation
    in constrained environments. It concentrates on four mechanism-level challenges: predictable
    tension transmission, dexterous motion with low distal mass, compliant distributed
    contact, and reliable operation under tight geometric and ergonomic constraints.
    Two prototype embodiments validate the proposed design framework. First, Lasso
    Gripper is a string-loop end-effector that forms an adaptive capture region via controlled
    launch-and-retract of a tensile element. We present the mechanical design, control
    strategy, and demonstration experiments that show the concept’s feasibility and
    practical advantages over conventional antipodal grippers: an extended capture envelope,
    distributed contact that reduces localized stress on soft objects, and robustness
    to positional uncertainty. The experiments reported are demonstration-focused and
    qualitative; they illustrate mechanism behaviors and common failure modes rather than
    providing exhaustive statistical metrics.
    Second, a compact handheld motorized laparoscopic instrument translates the same
    tendon-driven principles into a clinically constrained form factor. A benchtop prototype
    integrating proximal actuation, differential tendon routing, tension-preserving reels, and
    closed-loop control is described and validated in interaction and interface tests. Clinical
    evaluation are beyond this work’s scope and are left for future study.
    Together, these contributions establish mechanism-level design rules for tendondriven
    systems and demonstrate their transferability between general robotic grasping
    and minimally invasive surgical tools.
